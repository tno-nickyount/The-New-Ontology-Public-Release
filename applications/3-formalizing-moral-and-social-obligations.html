Formalizing Moral and Social Obligations

Grounding ethics and society in structural coherence

Introduction

The New Ontology defines reality as the resolution of structure across two interdependent domains:

  • The Empirical Domain, governed by force
  • The Aetherial Domain, governed by meaning

Moral and social obligations are not imposed from outside. They are the structural consequences of coherence and collapse.

This supplement invites interdisciplinary collaboration to formalize moral and social obligation within this dual-domain architecture, and to develop objective criteria for evaluating ethical actions, institutional integrity, and social coherence.

Reinterpreting Obligation in Structural Terms

Structural Foundations of Obligation

Moral obligation arises when meaning-bearing Forms resolve coherently in the presence of agency.

Actions that violate coherence are not merely imprudent. They are structurally wrong.

Ethics is not sentiment. It is fidelity to Form under constraint.

Wrongness is not failure. It is contradiction made manifest through action.

The F/S Ratio provides an evaluative metric: How much structural weight is borne by how much actual fidelity?

This metric grounds moral judgment in resolution—not emotion, tradition, or result.

Social Obligation as Systemic Coherence

Societies are systems—recursive structures seeking coherence over time. Their obligations are not invented. They emerge when shared norms sustain both presence and meaning.

Institutional behavior must cohere not only within the Empirical Domain (policy, action) but with the Aetherial (meaning, justice, trust).

Social obligation is the mapping of material behavior to ethical Form. Where that mapping fails, systems collapse—not always visibly, but structurally.

Toward Formalization: A Dual-Domain Framework

A. Ethical Language and Domain-Specific Predicates

Define Aetherial predicates:

  • Coheres(x)
  • Violates(x, F)
  • Obligated(x)
  • Fidelity(x, F)

Complement these with empirical observables:

  • Institutional stability
  • Social trust
  • Cooperation indices
  • Conflict emergence

Only when both domains instantiate coherently can we say an obligation has been fulfilled.

B. Resolution and Collapse in Ethical Inference

Moral Resolution Rule: If an action aligns with a resolved Form and its material expression does not contradict that Form, it is permissible.

Collapse Rule: If an action contradicts the Form it purports to instantiate—or generates structural incoherence—it fails, regardless of perceived success.

These rules formalize morality not as reaction, but as resolution.

C. Metrics and Assessment Tools

F/S Ratio for Ethics: Evaluate moral action by its fidelity to the structural weight it bears.

Simulation and Modeling: Model institutions as coherence-bearing systems. Test how policies, laws, and norms preserve or violate structural integrity.

Research and Collaboration

A. Interdisciplinary Workshops

  • What measurable behaviors correlate with resolved moral Forms?
  • How do we formally detect collapse in social systems?
  • What principles govern structural integrity across time and scale?

B. Pilot Studies and Modeling

Computational Ethics: Simulate agent interactions under structural constraints. Observe which patterns generate long-term coherence.

Organizational Case Studies: Test real-world policies for alignment with stated values. Measure whether structures resolve or contradict under pressure.

C. Publications and Grants

Develop interdisciplinary papers that model ethics as structural presence. Apply for joint funding to support simulation, measurement, and theoretical refinement.

Broader Implications

A. Beyond Cultural Relativism

Moral truth is not preference. It is resolution that holds under recursive pressure.

Different cultures may express Forms differently, But coherence is not relative.

B. Bridging Normative and Empirical

Ethics becomes testable—not because it becomes shallow, But because structure admits no contradiction.

This allows policy, law, and interpersonal behavior To be modeled not only by outcome—but by fidelity.

Conclusion

To act with moral fidelity is to act in a way that coheres.

This supplement offers a path to model that fidelity. Not with commandments, but with structure. Not with sentiment, but with resolution.

We call on ethicists, systems theorists, cognitive scientists, and modelers: Help us build the logic of obligation, The metrics of trust, And the systems that do not collapse.

Because coherence is not advice. It is what must hold, If anything is to remain.
