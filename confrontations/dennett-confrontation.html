Confrontation: Daniel Dennett

Intentional Stance, Illusionism, and the Aetherial Reality of Experience

I. Thinker

Daniel Dennett — A prominent figure in philosophy of mind, Dennett is known for his staunch physicalism and critiques of qualia. His work in Consciousness Explained and From Bacteria to Bach and Back presents consciousness as an emergent, non-substantial byproduct of neural processes and behavioral modeling.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Dennett argues that consciousness does not require metaphysical depth. Rather than being a “thing” that exists, it is an effect—a narrative construction that emerges from complex information processing. Concepts like qualia, subjectivity, or inner experience are dismissed as misfires of folk psychology. The mind is what the brain does, and experience is just what it’s like to simulate having one.

III. Claimed Consequences

If Dennett is right, then experience is not ontologically real—it is a convenient fiction. Consciousness has no privileged status; it is functionally modeled, not fundamentally instantiated. From this view, ethical, aesthetic, or introspective dimensions of life are evolutionary strategies, not structural realities.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology accepts that consciousness is instantiated in physical systems—but it rejects the claim that experience is therefore an illusion. Experience, defined as the presence of structure to itself, is not something added to physical processing—it is what makes that processing intelligible from within. Dennett collapses meaning into function, but function only exists relative to meaning. By denying the structural status of experience, Dennett commits a category error—treating the Aetherial as a psychological projection rather than a domain of structured reality. He eliminates what he has failed to define.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

We can accept Dennett’s insight that much of consciousness is constructed, and that brains are information-processing engines shaped by evolution. But construction does not negate instantiation. The stories we tell ourselves are not evidence against experience—they are artifacts of it. The New Ontology agrees that consciousness arises from structure—but it insists that structure is not limited to empirical matter. There is no simulation without a referent. The fact that experience is narratable proves it is structurally real.

VI. Verdict

  • Dennett reveals the constructed nature of many aspects of selfhood and cognition.
  • But he errs in treating experience as a trick rather than a structural resolution.
  • Illusion requires an experiencer.
  • The Aetherial Domain is not epiphenomenal—it is foundational.
  • Consciousness is not magic, but it is real.
  • To simulate experience, a system must instantiate meaning. And to instantiate meaning is to cross into the domain Dennett denies.
