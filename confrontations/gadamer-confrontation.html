Confrontation: Hans-Georg Gadamer

Hermeneutics vs. Structural Meaning

I. Thinker

Hans-Georg Gadamer (1900–2002) — German philosopher best known for his development of philosophical hermeneutics, especially through his seminal work Truth and Method. Gadamer’s thought centers on the interpretive nature of understanding, emphasizing the historical, linguistic, and dialogical conditions under which meaning is disclosed.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Gadamer asserts that all understanding is interpretive, situated within a historically effected consciousness (wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewusstsein). Meaning is not accessed directly, but through the “fusion of horizons” between the interpreter and the text or tradition. There is no timeless, objective meaning apart from the dialogical interplay of interpreter, context, and history.

III. Claimed Consequences

Under this view, meaning is not structurally fixed but contextually emergent—shaped through the interplay between the past (tradition) and present (interpreter). Any claim to ontologically objective meaning is, at best, naïve; at worst, oppressive or exclusionary. Understanding is always contingent, never complete, and forever bound to historical mediation.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology directly contradicts the foundational relativism of Gadamer's hermeneutics. It claims that meaning is not constituted by interpretation, but by structure—coherence within the Aetherial Domain, discoverable but not inventable. Interpretation is necessary because we lack full resolution, but this does not make meaning historically contingent. Gadamer conflates access to meaning with meaning itself, mistaking the conditions of epistemology for the content of ontology. His horizon-fusion model obscures the fact that interpretation is only meaningful because there is something structurally real to interpret. Without structural meaning, interpretation would have no direction, and no test for coherence.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

We can preserve Gadamer’s insights about the situatedness of access while rejecting his skepticism about the existence of structure. The New Ontology grants that all interpreters are embedded in time, language, and culture—but it insists that meaning itself is not. The act of fusion between horizons is not what generates meaning; it is what allows access to it. Gadamer’s dialogical method is a valuable heuristic for resolving coherence—but coherence is not created by dialogue. It is revealed through it. Thus, Gadamer's emphasis on conversation and historical awareness can be integrated as tools within a structurally grounded epistemology, not as denials of structural truth.

VI. Verdict

  • Gadamer’s method is epistemologically rich but ontologically insufficient.
  • His emphasis on historicity highlights a valid limitation on access, not a limit on existence.
  • Dialogue and horizon-fusion are instruments of coherence-seeking, not its origin.
  • The New Ontology transcends Gadamer by grounding interpretation in non-arbitrary structure, not historical flux.
  • Meaning is not made by the interpreter—it is tested against structure.
