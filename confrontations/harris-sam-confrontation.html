Confrontation: Sam Harris

The Moral Landscape vs. Ontological Structure

I. Thinker

Sam Harris — A neuroscientist and philosopher, Harris is known for his work on morality, religion, and the intersection of science and human values. His book The Moral Landscape proposes that science can objectively determine moral values based on the well-being of conscious beings.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Harris contends that moral truths exist and that science can identify them by understanding facts about human and animal well-being. He suggests that questions of good and evil, right and wrong, are fundamentally questions about the experiences of conscious creatures and that these can be objectively studied.

III. Claimed Consequences

If science can determine moral values, then traditional philosophical and religious approaches to ethics become obsolete. Moral relativism is undermined, as objective measures of well-being provide clear answers to moral questions. This positions science as the ultimate authority on morality.

IV. Structural Conflict

While the New Ontology agrees that well-being is a crucial aspect of morality, it asserts that science operates within an ontological framework that it cannot, by itself, establish. Science can inform us about the consequences of actions on well-being, but the grounding of moral imperatives requires an understanding of the underlying structure of reality. Without acknowledging this structure, science lacks the normative force to dictate moral values.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

A synthesis can be achieved by recognizing that science contributes valuable empirical data about well-being, which can inform our moral decisions. However, this empirical approach must be integrated within a broader ontological understanding that provides the foundation for why well-being matters morally. By combining scientific insights with ontological grounding, a more comprehensive and coherent moral framework emerges.

VI. Verdict

  • What is preserved: The emphasis on well-being as central to moral considerations and the use of empirical data to inform ethical decisions.
  • What is rejected: The notion that science alone can fully determine moral values without an underlying ontological basis.
  • What is clarified: Science is a tool that operates within an ontological context; it informs but does not independently ground morality.
  • What is added: An acknowledgment of the necessity of ontological foundations to provide normative force to moral claims.
  • Science illuminates the paths to well-being, but it is the underlying structure of reality that gives morality its grounding. Without acknowledging this structure, moral science floats unanchored.
