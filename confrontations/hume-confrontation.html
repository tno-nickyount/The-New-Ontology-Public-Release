Confrontation: David Hume

Empirical Skepticism vs. Aetherial Coherence

I. Thinker

David Hume (1711–1776) — A Scottish Enlightenment philosopher best known for his radical empiricism, his skepticism about causation, and his rejection of metaphysical necessity.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

All meaningful knowledge arises from sensory impressions or their combinations; ideas lacking empirical origin are suspect and should be discarded as meaningless. Causation is not a perceivable necessity, but a habit of thought—an association formed by repeated observations, not a metaphysical bond.

III. Claimed Consequences

Metaphysics collapses under scrutiny. Concepts such as necessary connection, the self, and moral truths are reduced to psychological habits or feelings. What cannot be traced to an empirical impression is either vacuous or the product of faulty reasoning.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology posits the Aetherial Domain as real but non-empirical: a domain governed by meaning rather than force, structure rather than observation. This includes non-observable structures like obligation, coherence, and contradiction. Hume’s standard of meaning—traceable to sense-data—would declare these invalid. But this critique itself relies on the structural reality of contradiction and coherence: Hume’s dismissal of metaphysics presumes the very conditions (non-empirical rules of inference, semantic constraints, internal consistency) that his framework refuses to acknowledge as real. Thus, his empiricism defeats itself the moment it asserts universal standards.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

One might suggest that the New Ontology accepts Hume’s critique of naive metaphysics but avoids the error by grounding non-empirical claims in structural necessity, not speculation. The Aetherial Domain does not float free—it is tethered by coherence. Where Hume sees unverifiable fictions, the New Ontology sees structurally constrained meaning. Hume's insights into the limits of observation are preserved; his denial of structural necessity is not.

VI. Verdict

  • Hume’s challenge is legitimate against metaphysical dogma, but not against structural realism.
  • The Aetherial Domain is not inferred but entailed—not based on impressions, but on the necessity of structure to intelligibility.
  • Hume's empiricism cannot justify itself without appealing to non-empirical structures like logic and coherence, which undermines its own standard of validity.
  • The New Ontology transcends Hume by preserving his epistemic humility while rejecting his metaphysical impoverishment.
