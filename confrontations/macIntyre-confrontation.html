Confrontation: Alasdair MacIntyre

Moral Tradition vs. Structural Obligation

I. Thinker

Alasdair MacIntyre is a moral philosopher best known for After Virtue, where he argues that modern ethical discourse has become incoherent due to the loss of shared moral traditions. Drawing on Aristotelian virtue ethics, he emphasizes the importance of historically embedded practices and community-based moral reasoning. His work critiques Enlightenment moral theories and seeks a return to narrative-based, tradition-grounded ethics.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Moral reasoning cannot stand on its own—it must be situated within a historical and cultural tradition of practice. Without that grounding, concepts like “virtue,” “justice,” or “good” lose their meaning and become little more than emotive placeholders or rhetorical tools.

III. Claimed Consequences

Because modern societies have lost coherent moral traditions, we now inhabit a post-ethical landscape—a world of emotivism and moral incommensurability. The solution, MacIntyre argues, is to revive thick, tradition-bound communities where virtues can be cultivated in continuity with past forms of life.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology rejects the claim that only tradition can supply coherence. Structural obligation is not a product of culture but of meaning itself. The Aetherial Domain—within which obligation exists as a matter of coherence, not consensus—predates and transcends any tradition. While traditions may approximate moral structure, they do not generate it. In this view, obligation does not emerge from narrative continuity—it is the very standard by which the coherence of narratives must be measured.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

There is partial overlap: tradition can be seen as a heuristic for preserving proximity to Aetherial coherence. MacIntyre is right to observe that lived practices shape moral understanding—but wrong to treat tradition as a source of truth. Instead, tradition should be understood as an adaptive structure—tested across time against the deeper, non-narrative structure of reality itself. When traditions remain coherent, they persist. When they contradict Aetherial structure, they degrade or collapse.

VI. Verdict

  • Right about the moral fragmentation of modernity.
  • Right about the role of practice in cultivating moral discernment.
  • Wrong to locate the source of moral truth within tradition.
  • Wrong to treat tradition as ontologically prior to obligation.
  • Corrective: The New Ontology provides a non-traditional grounding for obligation: coherence within the Aetherial Domain.
