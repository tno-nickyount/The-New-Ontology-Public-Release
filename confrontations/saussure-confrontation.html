Confrontation: Ferdinand de Saussure

Relational Meaning vs. Structural Meaning

I. Thinker

Ferdinand de Saussure was a Swiss linguist whose work at the turn of the 20th century redefined the study of language. He introduced the distinction between langue (the structured system of language) and parole (individual speech acts), and argued that meaning in language arises from differential relationships between signs. His model of the signifier and signified became foundational for structuralism, semiotics, and later postmodern thought.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Saussure held that the relationship between words and meanings is arbitrary, and that the structure of language is purely differential: words do not gain meaning through direct reference, but through opposition and contrast within a system. Language is a closed, self-referential system, and meaning arises from the pattern of relations, not from correspondence with external reality.

III. Claimed Consequence

If language is structurally arbitrary and meaning is relational, then all meaning becomes internal to systems of signs. This undermines any claim to fixed or stable reference, and dissolves the idea that language can “mirror” reality. The implication is that truth itself is a function of sign-relations, not of structural reality. From here, post-structuralism takes over—treating language as the final horizon of thought.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology accepts Saussure’s insight that meaning is structural—but challenges his confinement of structure to language. Meaning does not arise from words but from Forms, which exist in the Aetherial Domain independently of linguistic expression. Language is a tool for approximating Form—not constructing it. While signs operate differentially within a system, their referential capacity is anchored in something real: coherence within structure. Saussure was right to see structure as the key—but he misidentified the field in which it resides.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

Saussure’s differential model maps cleanly onto Aetherial structure once we decouple it from linguistic arbitrariness. His insight that meaning emerges from relational positioning anticipates our claim that meaning is structural coherence—not individual perception or semantic intention. Language is not the generator of meaning but a structurally governed medium through which Form is partially realized in the Empirical. Saussure glimpsed the scaffolding but mistook the blueprint for the building.

VI. Verdict

  • Right: Meaning is structural, not essential or referential.
  • Right: Meaning arises from differential coherence within systems.
  • Wrong: Language is the exclusive domain of meaning-formation.
  • Wrong: The arbitrariness of signs implies the arbitrariness of meaning itself.
  • Corrective: Structure precedes language. The Aetherial Domain is where meaning resides. Language does not create it—it attempts to resolve it.
