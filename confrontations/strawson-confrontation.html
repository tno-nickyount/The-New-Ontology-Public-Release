Confrontation: Galen Strawson

Basic Particulars vs. Ontological Participation

I. Thinker

Galen Strawson is an analytic philosopher known for his work in metaphysics, philosophy of mind, and personal identity. He coined the term “realistic monism” to describe the view that experience must be a fundamental part of reality, rejecting physicalism that excludes experiential properties. His argument against the “narrative self” challenges the idea that identity must be unified over time, proposing instead a view of personhood built from transient experiential fragments.

II. Central Philosophical Claims Challenged

  • Panpsychism: Conscious experience is a fundamental feature of the physical world, present in all matter to some degree.
  • Episodic Selfhood: There is no deep or diachronic self; rather, selves are short-lived, moment-bound configurations of experience.

III. Claimed Consequences

  • To understand consciousness, we must abandon purely materialist frameworks and accept that experience is baked into the fundamental structure of matter.
  • The self is not a continuous entity but a series of disjointed experiential episodes, loosely related through memory and proximity.

IV. Structural Conflict

The New Ontology agrees with Strawson on the ontological reality of experience, but rejects the idea that experience is necessarily located within matter. Instead, experience arises from cross-domain structural instantiation—a system structured enough to instantiate coherence across the Empirical and Aetherial Domains.

On the self, we diverge more sharply: Strawson’s episodic view misconstrues continuity as sameness. We assert that the self persists as a qualified structural coherence—not as substance or memory, but as recursive resolution. Identity is not a fiction—it is the coherence of structural passage through time.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

Strawson’s panpsychism can be reframed as an intuitive glimpse of the Aetherial Domain: he rightly sees that experience cannot be reduced to empirical mechanisms, but misidentifies it as a property of matter.

Similarly, his critique of the narrative self rightly identifies the dangers of retroactive storytelling, but throws out structural continuity with it. A coherent middle path exists: experience is not atomized, nor wholly unified—it is structured participation across time, tested by coherence, not memory.

VI. Verdict

  • Right: Experience is real and irreducible.
  • Right: Selfhood is not reducible to narrative fiction.
  • Wrong: Experience is not located in matter—rather, it arises from structured cross-domain coherence.
  • Wrong: To deny continuity of identity—structural persistence is not memory but coherence.
  • Corrective: The self is not an illusion. It is not a substance. It is a structurally coherent passage across time.
