Confrontation: Alfred Tarski

Semantic Truth vs. Aetherial Coherence

I. Thinker

Alfred Tarski was a Polish logician and mathematician whose most influential contribution is the semantic conception of truth. His T-schema aimed to clarify how truth operates in formal languages by linking linguistic statements to the world in a compositional and recursive way. He insisted on a deflationary, non-metaphysical account of truth: truth is a property of sentences that corresponds to facts, and nothing more.

II. Central Philosophical Claim Challenged

Truth is a linguistic property that emerges from the correspondence between statements and states of affairs. It is not metaphysical, not domain-transcendent, and does not require grounding outside formal or natural languages.

III. Claimed Consequences

  • A rigorous and self-contained system for defining truth eliminates ambiguity and avoids metaphysical confusion.
  • Truth becomes analyzable within formal systems and does not require appeal to external or ontological structures.

IV. Structural Conflict

Tarski treats truth as a function of language—our framework treats truth as a function of structure. Tarski's schema is powerful within its linguistic scope, but it fails to account for how truth operates across ontological domains. Under the New Ontology, truth is structural coherence—the degree to which a claim or system aligns with the governing structure of its domain (Empirical or Aetherial). This allows for truths that are not reducible to linguistic formulations. In our view, truth is not a predicate of language, but a measure of fidelity to domain structure.

V. Reconciliation Attempt

Tarski’s insights remain useful when truth must be rendered in formal or computational systems. His schema provides a syntactic heuristic for maintaining coherence within self-contained language games. But the New Ontology reframes this utility: the T-schema is not an account of truth, but a valid operational mechanism within the linguistic slice of structural reality. In that limited context, it holds. But it cannot be universalized. Structural truth—especially in the Aetherial Domain—requires alignment with coherence, not correspondence alone.

VI. Verdict

  • Right: To seek rigor in the definition of truth.
  • Right: To reject metaphysical vagueness in semantic theory.
  • Wrong: To treat truth as a purely linguistic function.
  • Wrong: To ignore domain structure as truth’s underlying ground.
  • Corrective: Truth is not a predicate of language—it is the structural alignment of a system or statement within its domain. Tarski’s schema is a tool; the New Ontology supplies the terrain it operates on.
